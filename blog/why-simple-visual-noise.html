Why we built Simple Visual Noise

Every ambient-sound app on the store ships a library of MP3s. We thought there was a more interesting answer.

Open the App Store and search "white noise". You'll see thirty apps, all variations of the same thing: a grid of preset cards, each one a 30-second sample looped. The interface is a volume slider. Maybe a timer. The premium tier costs $4 a month for access to more samples.

Sound is interesting. The app category that surrounds it is not.

What we wanted instead

Three things that don't exist together anywhere:

1. A synth, not a sample library. Every sample loops. Every loop has a seam your brain learns to hear over the first ten minutes. Procedural noise has no loop — α (the spectral tilt parameter) gives you a continuous palette from "super bright" to "deeper than brown", and every second is novel.

2. Premade visuals, never streamed AI. The mood-image generation category is exciting in the abstract and frustrating in practice — surprise outputs, slow latency, network dependence. We pre-rendered ten Krea moods, reviewed every one, and shipped them in the bundle. The mood layer is therefore deterministic, fast, and under our editorial control.

3. Generative composition without a subscription. Endel's pitch — "infinite personalized soundscapes" — turns out to be ~250 lines of code if you build it as a synthesis problem instead of an AI product. Per-layer modulators (sine, triangle, random-walk, beating) with phase offsets, evolving the mix over minutes. The composer is part of the app, not a service.

What we left behind

Subscriptions. Sample marketplaces. "Sleep stories" with celebrity voiceovers. Daily content drops. Account systems. Push notifications. Cloud personalization. We don't take any of those bets — they all push the app toward attention-economy churn and away from being a tool you forget about and trust.

A sound app should feel like a Casio digital watch: bought once, carried for a decade, never needs anything from you.

What's next

We're keeping the core sound engine simple and pushing depth into adjacent surfaces: the Apple Watch companion, Live Activity, spatial audio. The web demo on this site runs the same procedural engine, ported to WebAudio, so you can hear what the app does before buying it. If that demo doesn't sell the idea — we built the wrong app.

— William Jia Min, founder
